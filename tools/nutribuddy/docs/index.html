🧠 Core Concepts

Understand the science and theory before diving into the application.

What is Electrical Conductivity (EC)?

EC measures the total dissolved nutrients (salts) in your solution. It's measured in millisiemens per centimeter (mS/cm).

  • Low EC (0.8–1.2): Few nutrients; good for seedlings to avoid burn
  • Medium EC (1.2–2.0): Moderate nutrition; suitable for most growth stages
  • High EC (2.0–2.8+): Dense nutrition; for blooming/fruiting or high-demand crops

Rule of thumb: Start low, increase gradually. It's easier to add than remove nutrients.

The NPK Macro-Triad

N-P-K are the three primary macronutrients your plants need most:

  • Nitrogen (N): Drives leaf and stem growth; essential for vegetative stages
  • Phosphorus (P): Critical for root development and flower/fruit formation
  • Potassium (K): Strengthens plants, improves stress tolerance, enhances fruit quality

N-P-K ratios shift based on crop and growth stage. Vegetative stages want high N; flowering wants low N, high P-K.

Why A/B Split Solutions?

One core chemistry principle: calcium and sulfate cannot coexist in high concentrations without precipitating.

Chemistry: Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → CaSO₄↓ (insoluble, forms crystals)

To avoid this:

  • Part A (Calcium): Contains calcium nitrate + other cations (K, Mg) + micronutrients
  • Part B (Sulfate/Phosphate): Contains potassium sulfate, phosphate, additional magnesium sulfate

They're mixed separately in water, then combined only at final dilution (1:100 ratio).

Macronutrients vs. Micronutrients

Macronutrients (needed in large quantities):

  • Primary: N, P, K (often 50–300 ppm each)
  • Secondary: Ca, Mg, S (often 40–150 ppm each)

Micronutrients (needed in tiny quantities):

  • B, Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, Mo, Ni (typically 0.05–2.0 ppm each)
  • Imbalance is rare unless you use well water with unusual mineral content

pH and Nutrient Availability

Plant roots absorb nutrients best at specific pH ranges. Most hydroponic crops prefer pH 5.5–6.5:

  • Too acidic (pH < 5.0): Iron, manganese, copper become toxic; calcium, magnesium become deficient
  • Too basic (pH > 7.0): Iron, manganese, zinc become deficient; lockout occurs

Best practice: Measure pH; adjust with phosphoric acid (lower) or potassium hydroxide (raise).

Stock Concentration Ratios

NutriCalc assumes a 100× concentrate — meaning you mix concentrated solutions, then dilute 1:100 for the final reservoir:

  • 30 mL concentrate + 2,970 mL water = 3 L of final solution

Why concentrates?

  • Easier to store (takes up less space)
  • Longer shelf life (concentrated solutions resist degradation)
  • Reproducible (scales proportionally)

You can adjust strength:

  • 50× concentrate: Larger volumes, easier to measure, less precise
  • 100× concentrate: Balance of convenience and precision (recommended)
  • 200× concentrate: Tiny volumes, requires precise scales, higher precision

Compatibility & Precipitation Risks

NutriCalc warns about five major compatibility issues:

Risk Cause Solution
Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ precipitation CaSO₄ crystals form Use A/B split (automatic)
Ca²⁺ + PO₄³⁻ precipitation Ca₃(PO₄)₂ crystals Reduce Ca or P slightly
Iron solubility loss Fe oxidizes at high pH Lower pH to 5.5; use chelated Fe
NH₄⁺ toxicity High ammonium > 10% of N Use nitrate-based nitrogen sources
Boric acid solubility Boric acid precipitates > 4.7 g/L Limit boron to 2.0 ppm or less

The NutriCalc Solver (How It Works)

Behind the scenes, NutriCalc uses Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) — a mathematical technique that:

  1. Takes your target nutrient levels (ppm)
  2. Looks at available chemicals in your library
  3. Solves a system of equations to find the exact quantities of each chemical
  4. Reports element deviations so you know accuracy

Why SVD? Because there's usually no exact solution — chemicals have multiple nutrients. SVD finds the best approximation.

Cost Optimization

NutriCalc tracks chemical costs to help you find the cheapest formulation that still meets your targets.

  • Different chemicals have different costs
  • Cheaper sources may exist; prices vary by region and supplier
  • Update prices regularly in the Chemical Library for accurate cost tracking

⚡ Quickstart Guide

Get your first formula mixed in 5 minutes.

Step 1: Set Your Target

Open the Formula Builder page and set your EC (Electrical Conductivity) target.

💡 Tip: Start with EC 1.8 mS/cm for leafy greens, EC 2.0 for tomatoes.

Step 2: Choose a Preset

Select a crop-specific formula from the Quick Presets:

  • Lettuce — Seedling: High nitrogen, low EC
  • Lettuce — Grow: Balanced formula for vegetative growth
  • Tomato — Vegetative: Moderate nitrogen, higher potassium
  • Tomato — Bloom/Fruit: Reduced nitrogen, high phosphorus & potassium

Step 3: Fine-Tune Sliders

Adjust the nutrient sliders to customize the formula for your specific crop and growth stage:

  • Macronutrients: N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S (use to balance ratios)
  • Micronutrients: B, Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, Mo (usually kept at standard levels)
ℹ️ Info: Adjust in small increments (±5-10%) to avoid major imbalances.

Step 4: Solve & Review

Click Solve to generate the formula. Review the results:

  • EC Estimate: Should match your target (±0.2)
  • Element Deviation: Bars should be mostly green (±10%)
  • Compatibility Alerts: Check for any warnings (yellow/red)

Step 5: Mix Your Batch

Go to the Production page to:

  1. View the Bill of Materials with chemical quantities
  2. Follow the Mixing Guide step-by-step
  3. Complete the Verification Checklist before using
⚠️ Important: Always follow the A/B split — mix Part A (Calcium) and Part B (Sulfate/Phosphate) separately to prevent precipitation.

📖 User Guide

Complete reference for NutriCalc features and workflows.

Overview

NutriCalc is a hydroponic nutrient calculator designed to help growers formulate precise, balanced solutions for any crop and growth stage. The tool combines:

  • Scientific solver: Uses SVD-based mathematics to calculate exact chemical quantities
  • Safety alerts: Warns about precipitation risk, solubility limits, and nutrient toxicity
  • Cost tracking: Estimates production cost per batch
  • Production workflows: Guided mixing steps, verification checklists, and supplier BOM

Formula Builder

EC Target

Electrical Conductivity (EC) measures the total dissolved nutrients in your solution:

  • 0.8–1.2 mS/cm: Seedlings, weak plants (recovery)
  • 1.2–1.8 mS/cm: Leafy greens, herbs
  • 1.8–2.2 mS/cm: Fruiting crops (tomatoes, cucumbers)
  • 2.2–2.8 mS/cm: High-demand crops (strawberries, peppers)

Nutrient Sliders

Adjust each element in ppm (parts per million):

  • Nitrogen (N): Drives vegetative growth; reduce during bloom
  • Phosphorus (P): Essential for root and flower development
  • Potassium (K): Improves stress tolerance and fruit quality
  • Calcium (Ca): Structural nutrient; prevent Ca deficiency in high-demand crops
  • Magnesium (Mg): Core of chlorophyll; 10–15% of N level
  • Sulfur (S): Often overlooked but important; usually 10–20% of N

Micronutrients

Boron, Iron, Manganese, Zinc, Copper, Molybdenum, Nickel. Start with standard levels and adjust only if symptoms appear.

Nutrient Profile

Radar Chart

Element Deviation

Shows percentage deviation from target:

  • Green (±10%): Excellent match
  • Yellow (±20%): Acceptable, but consider re-solving
  • Red (>±20%): Significant deviation; try different compounds

Nutrient Ratio Analysis

Compares your formula to scientific reference frames (Bugbee, Hoagland). Slight deviations are OK for crop-specific optimization.

A/B Stock Split

Why split? Calcium (Ca²⁺) + Sulfate (SO₄²⁻) form insoluble calcium sulfate (CaSO₄) when mixed together. By keeping them in separate containers:

  • Part A: Contains calcium nitrate, potassium, magnesium, and micronutrients
  • Part B: Contains potassium sulfate, phosphate, and additional sulfate

Mix Part A first in water, then Part B. Never pre-mix dry Part A + Part B together.

Production Workflow

Bill of Materials

Shows exact quantities of each chemical needed. Assumes a 100× concentrate (diluted 1:100 in final reservoir):

  • For 50× concentrate, divide all volumes by 2
  • For 200× concentrate, multiply all volumes by 2

Mixing Guide

Step-by-step instructions:

  1. Prepare: Clean containers, measure water temperature, gather chemicals
  2. Part A (Calcium): Dissolve in water first, stir until fully dissolved
  3. Part B (Sulfate/Phosphate): Dissolve in separate water, stir until fully dissolved
  4. Store: Label containers, keep in cool dark place
  5. Dilute: Add both parts to final reservoir at 1:100 ratio
⚠️ Critical: Never mix Part A and Part B dry chemicals together. Precipitation will ruin your solution.

Verification Checklist

Before using your solution, complete these tests:

  1. pH Test: Use calibrated pH meter; target is crop-specific (usually 5.5–6.5)
  2. EC Test: Confirm EC matches your target (±0.2 mS/cm tolerance)
  3. Appearance: No cloudiness, precipitate, or discoloration
  4. Smell: No unusual odor indicating decomposition
  5. Temperature: Room temperature (18–25°C); cold solutions reduce absorption

Chemical Alerts

NutriCalc checks for these compatibility issues:

  • Calcium + Sulfate precipitation: Avoids by A/B split design
  • Calcium + Phosphate precipitation: Warns if high levels
  • Iron solubility: Unchelated iron oxidizes at pH > 5.5
  • Ammonium toxicity: Warns if >10% of total nitrogen
  • Boron solubility: Warns if >4.7 g/L (boric acid limit)

Cost Tracking

NutriCalc estimates production cost per batch based on chemical prices. Prices come from:

  • Supplier APIs (if configured)
  • Manual entry (Chemical Library page)

Use cost comparison to find the cheapest blend without sacrificing quality.

Dosing & MQTT

For automated greenhouse control, configure MQTT (Message Queuing Telemetry Transport):

  1. Set broker address and port (usually localhost:1883 for local server)
  2. Map each compound to a physical pump number
  3. Click "Publish" to send dosing instructions to all pumps
ℹ️ Advanced: Requires MQTT-compatible pump controllers (e.g., ESP32 with PubSubClient).

🔧 Troubleshooting

Common problems and solutions.

Formula Issues

❌ "Solver failed" or "No solution found"

Problem: NutriCalc cannot formulate your target with the selected chemicals.

Solutions:

  • Add more chemical options (Chemical Library page)
  • Lower EC target slightly (sometimes high EC + specific elements conflict)
  • Adjust nutrient ratios (some target combos are mathematically impossible)
  • Use a preset formula as a baseline; then tweak

❌ Element deviation is red (>±20%)

Problem: One or more elements miss the target significantly.

Solutions:

  • Try different compounds for that element
  • Adjust EC target ±0.2 mS/cm to find a better balance
  • Check if compound is missing from library; add it
  • Accept slight deviation if agronomically acceptable (expert users only)

❌ "Chemical incompatibility warning"

Problem: Precipitation risk detected.

Solutions:

  • Calcium + Sulfate: Use A/B split (automatic in NutriCalc)
  • Calcium + Phosphate: Reduce calcium or phosphorus slightly
  • Iron at high pH: Lower pH to 5.5 or switch to chelated iron
  • Boron > 4.7 g/L: Use boric acid carefully; may precipitate

Mixing Issues

❌ Solution is cloudy (white/gray)

Problem: Precipitation has occurred.

Cause: Usually calcium sulfate (CaSO₄) — indicates A/B parts were mixed together before dilution.

Solution:

  • Discard the batch
  • Follow A/B split strictly: Part A (Ca) separate, Part B (SO₄) separate
  • Only mix when diluting to final reservoir

❌ EC is too high or too low

Problem: Measured EC doesn't match target.

Causes & solutions:

  • Tap water EC: If tap water is not DI/RO, EC will be higher. Measure your source water.
  • Measuring error: Calibrate meter with known solutions; check temperature compensation
  • Concentrate ratio wrong: Confirm 100× dilution (1 part concentrate : 99 parts water)
  • Evaporation: Water evaporates before nutrient, raising EC; top-up with DI/RO water

❌ pH is way off (too acidic or alkaline)

Problem: pH doesn't match target after mixing.

Solutions:

  • Too acidic (< 5.0): Add potassium hydroxide (KOH) in small amounts OR reduce nitrate salts
  • Too alkaline (> 7.0): Add phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) or nitric acid (HNO₃) in small amounts
  • Tap water pH issue: Measure source water pH; some water is naturally basic
  • Meter error: Calibrate pH meter with pH 7.0 and pH 4.0 standards

App Issues

❌ Formulas not saving

Problem: Click "Save" but formula doesn't appear in Saved Formulas.

Solutions:

  • Check browser console (F12 → Console) for errors
  • Clear browser cache and reload
  • Try a different browser
  • Ensure browser allows LocalStorage / cookies

❌ MQTT publish fails

Problem: "No ACK received" or publish times out.

Solutions:

  • Verify broker is running: mosquitto -v on Linux/Mac
  • Check broker address and port (default: localhost:1883)
  • Ensure pump controllers are subscribed to the topic
  • Check firewall rules allow MQTT traffic
  • Use a MQTT client (e.g., mosquitto_pub) to test connection

❌ Prices not updating

Problem: Chemical prices are stuck or showing "—".

Solutions:

  • Click "Refresh Prices" on the Chemical Library page
  • Check "Price Sources" — ensure you have API keys configured (if required)
  • Manually add prices: edit each compound in the library
  • Some chemicals may not have public pricing; you'll need to enter manually

Crop-Specific Issues

Yellowing leaves (nitrogen deficiency)

Solution: Increase nitrogen slightly, re-solve, and remix.

Purple/dark leaves (phosphorus deficiency)

Solution: Increase phosphorus, re-solve, and remix.

Weak stems (potassium deficiency)

Solution: Increase potassium, re-solve, and remix.

Interveinal chlorosis (magnesium deficiency)

Solution: Increase magnesium, re-solve, and remix.

Still Stuck?

If you can't find a solution:

  • Check error messages in browser console (F12)
  • Review the User Guide section relevant to your issue
  • Double-check A/B split and mixing procedure
  • Test your EC meter and pH meter with known standards
